## Introduction

## 1) Called

John 1:35-42
35 The next day again John was standing with two of his disciples, 36 and he looked at Jesus as he walked by and said, “Behold, the Lamb of God!” 37 The two disciples heard him say this, and they followed Jesus. 38 Jesus turned and saw them following and said to them, “What are you seeking?” And they said to him, “Rabbi” (which means Teacher), “where are you staying?” 39 He said to them, “Come and you will see.” So they came and saw where he was staying, and they stayed with him that day, for it was about the tenth hour. 40 One of the two who heard John speak and followed Jesus was Andrew, Simon Peter's brother. 41 He first found his own brother Simon and said to him, “We have found the Messiah” (which means Christ). 42 He brought him to Jesus. Jesus looked at him and said, “You are Simon the son of John. You shall be called Cephas” (which means Peter). 

Here we meet Andrew, who is identified as disciple of John the Baptist.  As such he has been following John into the wilderness, participating in baptisms, living a disciplined life of prayer and fasting, and spiritually searching for the Messiah.  He was living a life that was preparing him for the coming of the Messiah.  A life of simplicity, moral purity, and repentance.   Andrew is with John the Baptist when Jesus comes to him and he hears John say "Behold the lamb of God!"  So, there is a declaration that causes the spiritually attuned to turn towards Christ.  And, after spending some time with Him, Andrew's first action is to run and bring his family into the presence of the savior.

Now this immediately pays benefits because when the man known as Simon meets Jesus, his life is changed.  In contrast to Andrew, Simon was a man just trying to make a living.  He wasn't looking for a Messiah, he was looking to put food on his table and provide for his family, like his father before him.  He's doesn't appear much like his brother at all, he's not living a life in submission.  This must have been an extremely odd situation for the man known as Simon.  He meets Jesus and without so much as a hello, hi, how are you doing, he is identified by Jesus and renamed.

It was not a common practice of the time to just rename strangers; no more than it is today.  However, you meet people from time to time that will, in an effort to break ice or develop a rapport with you, pick something about you and make a name of it....
As uncommon as it may be, Jesus, in this moment, sees something in the man known as Simon that causes him to speak to Simon's greater purpose.  In calling him Cephas, which is later translated to Petros or Peter, which means The Rock, he's speaking destiny before the development, it's a purpose spoken before the preparation ever began.

Following this, we have a scenes that, according to Luke also happen before the call where, first, Jesus goes to Peter's home and heals his ailing mother-in-law.  This is important because has been introduced to Peter, but now he is revealing himself and he starts working wonders in Peter's life.  He meet's Peter along with his brother in the midst of his vocation and he tells them where to cast the net.  They catch more fish than can even fit in their boat.  And what I understand is happening here is that Jesus is taking care of the home.  Before he tells Peter and Andrew to rise up and leave everything behind, he makes sure the necessities are taken care of.  So, when Jesus later asks Peter and Andrew to put down their nets and follow him, they have personally witnessed the miracles.  Their homes have been made stable.  And, it's not a large leap for them to step out on faith.  However, what we've witnessed is Jesus calling and ordinary man to a, yet unrevealed and extraordinary purpose.  Peter steps in to a new identity as a disciple and a new destiny.

If God is calling you to something and you're right now wondering - How Sway?  You've seen God perform miracles in your life, he's given you the stability..... If He did it before, He can do it again, Same God right now, Same God back then!


## 2) Conflicted

Matthew 14:28-31
28 And Peter answered him, “Lord, if it is you, command me to come to you on the water.” 29 He said, “Come.” So Peter got out of the boat and walked on the water and came to Jesus. 30 But when he saw the wind, he was afraid, and beginning to sink he cried out, “Lord, save me.” 31 Jesus immediately reached out his hand and took hold of him, saying to him, “O you of little faith, why did you doubt?” 

Luke 22:31-34
31 “Simon, Simon, behold, Satan demanded to have you, that he might sift you like wheat, 32 but I have prayed for you that your faith may not fail. **And when you have turned again, strengthen your brothers.**” 33 Peter said to him, “Lord, I am ready to go with you both to prison and to death.” 34 Jesus said, “I tell you, Peter, the rooster will not crow this day, until you deny three times that you know me.”

One of the things I appreciate about several men and women of the Bible and especially about Peter today, is their humanity.  Peter, I think, is widely seen as a man that swings between faith and fear.  You might say, "Well, hold up minister, I thought you said Jesus called him The Rock."  He's got a strength inside of him that is built for the road ahead but his doubt early on won't let him fully surrender to Christ.  No matter how much he witnesses and experiences with Christ, his doubt is holding him back.  It's evident when he's called to walk on the water with Jesus.  Peter is bold enough to call out to what they thought was an apparition, but his doubt causes him to sink when he begins walking on the water.

In Luke, Jesus makes it known that satan wanted Peter bad, which I think speaks to how fragile Peter was on the inside.  The very words from his mouth spoke with certainty and assurance, confidence and courage but betrayed his true nature.  "Lord, I am ready to go with you both to prison and to death."  When in truth, he wasn't ready for either.  The only thing that was certain is that the right amount of pressure was going to cause him to buckle.  Both satan and Jesus saw the weakness, but the lens through which weakness is perceived matters.  One perceived his weakness through the eyes of ruin and the other through the eyes of grace.

Donny McClurkin used to sing a song "We fall down but we get up."  He said "for a saint is just a sinner who fell down and got up."  Peter was always on the verge of falling down, one character trait that we can't miss about Peter, as a fisherman, is that he was used investing time and energy in a thing and sometimes coming up empty handed.  He was used to going to bed frustrated but waking up in the morning and starting again.  Peter was a man that was familiar with a certain amount of failure.  He could take it.  But Jesus also tells us in this one sentence "And when you have turned again, strengthen your brothers and sisters" that he knew Peter had some bounce back in his spirit; that, like any good fighter, Peter had a round 2, round 3, 4, and so on in him.

Well, that sounds good and all preacher, but it doesn't sound much like a Rock.  I hear you but let's move forward.

So, in the garden of Gethsemane, the pressure begins to be applied.  You see Peter in the midst of the scuffle at Jesus's arrest, cuts off a soldiers ear and is rebuked by Jesus.  Some would say Peter shows courage in this moment.  Ok, but then he flips.  And after Jesus's arrest, he only follows from a distance because, here comes that fear again.  He's torn between devotion and self preservation.  And his 3 denials before the cock crows lead to phase 3.


## 3) Crushed - The Collapse of Self-Confidence
Peter’s greatest failure becomes the turning point of his transformation.

- **Peter’s inner conflict finally breaks under pressure**
    
    - Everything from Phase 2 converges here:
    
    - The man who walked on water now sinks in fear.
    - The man who swung a sword now trembles before a servant girl.
    - The man who vowed loyalty now follows at a distance.
    
    - Peter’s collapse is not sudden — it is the culmination of unresolved conflict

- **Jesus turns and looks at Peter — the moment that breaks him**
    
    - Only Luke records this detail.
    - Jesus is being beaten, yet He turns to find Peter in the crowd.
    - It is not a look of condemnation — it is a look of truth, love, and prophecy fulfilled.
    - Peter realizes:
    
    - Jesus knew him better than he knew himself.
    - His strength was an illusion.
    - His loyalty was weaker than his fear.
    
    - _Scripture:_ Luke 22:61

- **Peter weeps bitterly — the crushing of pride**
    
    - Peter runs out into the night and breaks down.
    - This is the lowest moment of his life — the death of self‑reliance.
    - The man who thought he was the strongest discovers he is the weakest.
    - He's not even there when Jesus is crucified.  What happened to prison or death?
    - But this breaking is not the end — it is the beginning of transformation.
    - _Scripture:_ Luke 22:62
- Peter’s failure is not random — it is the inevitable crash of a divided heart.
- His denial is not the end — it is the breaking God uses to rebuild him.
- His collapse is not disqualification — it is preparation for restoration


## 4) Commissioned
Thankfully Jesus restores broken people and entrusts them with His mission

- **The resurrection announcement — Peter runs toward hope**
    
    - The women report the empty tomb.
    - Peter and John run to the tomb; John arrives first, but Peter goes _in_ first.
    - Even in failure, Peter’s heart still moves toward Jesus.
    - _Scripture:_ Luke 24:9–12; John 20:3–8
- **“Do you love Me?” — Jesus restores Peter’s identity**
    
    - Jesus asks Peter three times, mirroring Peter’s three denials.
    - Jesus is not shaming Peter — He is healing him.
    - Each time Peter affirms his love, Jesus gives him a commission:
    
    - “Feed My lambs.”
    - “Tend My sheep.”
    - “Feed My sheep.”
    
    - Jesus is saying:
    
    - _Your failure does not disqualify you._
    - _Your calling is still intact._
    - _Your future is greater than your collapse._
    
    - _Scripture:_ John 21:15–17
